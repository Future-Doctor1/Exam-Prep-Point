Introduction to Critical Thinking in MDCAT

Critical thinking is not a subject you "know" — it's a skill you develop by practicing. MDCAT tests your ability to think logically about real-world statements, evaluate arguments, and identify what's being assumed but not stated. The MDCAT Logical Reasoning Unit 1 Live Test covers all these skills in the exact format you'll see on exam day.

Critical thinking in MDCAT differs from pure logic puzzles . While Unit 5 tests formal syllogisms and Venn diagrams, Unit 1 asks you to evaluate statements about the real world — statements with incomplete information, implicit assumptions, and competing arguments. Your job is to decide what follows logically and what doesn't.

Three core skills drive Unit 1 success:

  • Logical inference: deriving valid conclusions from given statements
  • Argument evaluation: judging whether an argument is strong or weak
  • Assumption identification: spotting what the author assumes without stating

Logical Inference & Deduction

Logical inference is the process of drawing a conclusion from premises. In MDCAT, inference questions give you a passage or statement and ask: "What can we conclude from this?" Your answer options have specific names:

Answer Type Meaning Example
Definitely true Must be true; logically certain from the passage "All birds lay eggs. A parrot is a bird." → Parrot lays eggs (definite).
Probably true Likely true, but not certain; exceptions could exist "Most engineers earn well." → An engineer probably earns well (but not guaranteed).
Data inadequate Cannot tell from the given info; need more data "Ali is rich." Can't conclude whether Ali is happy or not (need more data).
Probably false Unlikely, but not ruled out entirely "Few people in Pakistan like snow." It's probably false that most Pakistanis like snow.
Definitely false Must be false; contradicts the passage "All cats are mammals." → It's definitely false that cats are reptiles.

Example: Statement Analysis

Statement: "Ahmed's company has been successful for 5 years. Reported profits have risen each year, with 2018 being the strongest. Ahmed treated his staff to a meal at a 5-star restaurant."

Conclusion: "Ahmed is rich."

Analysis: The passage tells us Ahmed's company is successful and he paid for a nice meal — both consistent with wealth. But "successful company" doesn't guarantee Ahmed personally is rich (he might reinvest profits, carry debt, etc.). The answer is Probably true, not Definitely true.

Statement Analysis & Truth Values

MDCAT often gives you a passage with multiple statements and asks which conclusions follow. The key is reading for logical relationships, not just content.

Common Statement Patterns

  • "Some X are Y" — At least one X is Y; others may or may not be.
  • "All X are Y" — Every X belongs to Y; no exceptions.
  • "No X is Y" — X and Y have no overlap; mutually exclusive.
  • "X is healthier than Y" — Establishes a ranking; can be chained (A > B > C means A > C).
High-yield fact: "Some" does NOT mean "some but not all." In logic, "Some X are Y" is true even if ALL X are Y. Test this understanding on every "some" question.

Example: Ranking Statements

Given: "Cholesterol is healthier than saturated fat. Saturated fat is healthier than cyanide but less healthy than unsaturated fat."

Order: Unsaturated Fat > Saturated Fat > Cyanide, and Cholesterol > Saturated Fat.

The passage does NOT tell us where Cholesterol ranks versus Unsaturated Fat. If asked "Cholesterol is healthier than unsaturated fat," the answer is Data inadequate — we don't know this relationship.

Argument Evaluation & Strength

Argument evaluation questions present a scenario, then two or more proposed courses of action or arguments. You must decide which are "strong" (relevant, sound, supported) and which are "weak" (irrelevant, unsupported, easily refuted).

What Makes an Argument Strong?

  • Directly addresses the question — relevant to the issue at hand
  • Based on solid reasoning — logical connection between premise and conclusion
  • Resists counterargument — not easily disproven
  • Considers the full context — acknowledges complexities

What Makes an Argument Weak?

  • Tangential or irrelevant to the question
  • Relies on unproven assumptions
  • Ignores obvious counterarguments
  • Appeals to emotion rather than logic

Example: Pollution Control

Question: "Can pollution be controlled?"

Argument I (for control): "If everyone realizes the hazards and cooperates, pollution may be controlled."

Strength: Weak. It's vague (who is "everyone"?), assumes perfect cooperation (unrealistic), and doesn't explain HOW to achieve that cooperation.

Argument II (against control): "Crowded highways, factories, industries, and an ever-growing population eager for land to build are beyond control."

Strength: Stronger. It identifies concrete obstacles (infrastructure growth, population density) that genuinely resist easy solutions.

Correct answer: Either I or II is strong or Both are strong — depends on whether you find Argument I convincing. Most test-takers rate both as weak or mixed strength.

Identifying Implicit Assumptions

An implicit assumption is something the author takes for granted without stating it. To identify implicit assumptions, ask: "What must be TRUE for this statement to make sense?"

Example: Education & Exams

Statement: "The Education Minister stressed the need to stop the present examination system and replace it with methods that would measure the real merit of students."

What's being assumed?

  • Implicit Assumption I: "Examinations should be abolished."
  • Implicit Assumption II: "The present examination system does not measure real merit."

For the Minister's proposal to make sense, Assumption II must be true — if exams DID measure merit, there'd be no reason to replace them. Assumption I is stated outright ("stop the system"), so it's not implicit; it's explicit.

Answer: Only Assumption II is implicit.

Test strategy: For each assumption option, ask: "If this were FALSE, would the statement still stand?" If the answer is "no," the assumption is implicit.

Cause-Effect Relationships

MDCAT gives you two statements and asks: "What is the relationship between them?"

Relationship Type Definition Example
I causes II Statement I is the reason; Statement II is the outcome Increased coffee sales (I) → More coffee shops open (II)
II causes I Statement II is the reason; Statement I is the outcome Reduced sugar (I) ← Health awareness (II)
Both independent causes Both statements occur but neither causes the other Company expands (I) AND employees get pay raises (II) — separate decisions
Effects of common cause Both are results of a third, unstated factor Tobacco sales increase (I) AND smoker numbers decrease (II) ← Common cause: exports to other countries increase

Example: Tobacco & Smoking

Statement I: "The total amount of tobacco sold by tobacco-farmers has increased."

Statement II: "The number of adults who smoke has decreased."

Analysis: Both statements occur, but Statement II doesn't cause Statement I (fewer smokers wouldn't increase tobacco sales). Statement I doesn't cause Statement II either. The most likely explanation is a common cause: tobacco exports have increased — more tobacco sold overseas, but fewer domestic smokers.

Answer: Both statements are effects of a common cause.

Common MDCAT Mistakes in Critical Thinking

  1. Confusing "Probably true" with "Definitely true": Just because something is likely doesn't make it certain. Expect counterexamples.
  2. Assuming "Some" means "some but not all": In logic, "Some X are Y" is true even if all X are Y. Don't over-interpret.
  3. Falling for emotional appeals: Strong arguments are logical, not emotional. An argument about "saving lives" is weak if it ignores practical costs.
  4. Overlooking implicit assumptions: Read between the lines. What is the author taking for granted?
  5. Mixing up cause-effect relationships: Two things can both increase without one causing the other. Always consider common causes.
  6. Ignoring "Data inadequate" as an option: If you truly can't conclude something from the passage, that's the right answer — don't guess.
  7. Forcing conclusions that aren't logical: Just because something sounds reasonable doesn't make it a valid inference from the passage.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between logical inference and critical thinking?

Logical inference is deriving a conclusion from premises using valid reasoning. Critical thinking is broader — it includes inference but also argument evaluation, assumption spotting, and judgment about evidence quality. MDCAT Unit 6 tests critical thinking (analysis and evaluation), while Unit 5 tests pure logical inference (syllogisms).

How do you identify implicit assumptions in MDCAT questions?

An implicit assumption is unstated but necessary for the argument to work. To find it: (1) Read the claim carefully, (2) Ask "What must be true for this to make sense?", (3) Test each option by asking "If this were FALSE, would the claim still hold?" If the answer is "no," it's an implicit assumption.

What's the difference between "Definitely true" and "Probably true"?

"Definitely true" means logically certain from the passage — no exceptions or alternatives exist. "Probably true" means likely but not certain — exceptions are possible. If you can think of any reasonable scenario where the conclusion doesn't hold, it's "probably true," not "definitely true."

How do you evaluate whether an argument is strong or weak?

A strong argument is relevant, logical, and difficult to counter. A weak argument ignores the question, relies on assumptions, or is easily refuted. For MDCAT: ask "Does this directly support the claim?" and "Are there obvious counterarguments?" If both answers are "yes," the argument is weak.

What is the difference between cause-and-effect and independent causes?

Cause-effect means one statement directly causes the other — one explains the other. Independent causes means both occur but neither causes the other — they're separate events. If both are results of a hidden third factor, they're "effects of a common cause." Identifying this relationship is crucial for MDCAT reasoning.

Why do MDCAT questions often have "Data inadequate" as the right answer?

MDCAT tests your ability to recognize when you don't have enough information to conclude something. Jumping to conclusions without sufficient evidence is a critical thinking error. "Data inadequate" is correct when the passage doesn't provide the facts needed to answer definitively.
