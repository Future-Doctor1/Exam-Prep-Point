Why Take a Second Mock Test at All?

One mock test tells you where you stood on a single day. A second one tells you something far more useful: whether you're actually improving. This is the core idea behind spaced, repeated retrieval practice — a well-documented learning strategy summarized by the American Psychological Association, which finds that repeated self-testing over time builds stronger, longer-lasting recall than a single study or test session ever could.

Practically, comparing Mock Test 1 and Mock Test 2 scores lets you answer three questions:

  • Did my weak subject actually get stronger? Compare your subject-wise percentage between both attempts.
  • Is my pacing improving? Check whether you finished with more time to spare, or less panic in the final 30 minutes.
  • Are the same topics tripping me up? If a mistake repeats across both tests, that's a concept gap — not bad luck.

How to Use Mock Test 2 Effectively

  1. Simulate real conditions. Sit somewhere quiet, keep your phone away, and don't pause the timer — the goal is to feel exam-day pressure now, not on 16 August.
  2. Attempt every question. Since MDCAT 2026 has no negative marking, an educated guess is always better than a blank answer.
  3. Review before you compare scores. Go through the full answer review first. Understand *why* an answer was wrong before looking at your percentage.
  4. Log your subject-wise breakdown. Keep a simple note of your Biology, Chemistry, and Physics percentages from both Mock Test 1 and Mock Test 2 side by side.
