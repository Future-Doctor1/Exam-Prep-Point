Introduction to Atomic Structure

Atomic structure is the foundation of all chemistry. Understanding how electrons arrange themselves around a nucleus determines an atom's chemical properties, reactivity, and how it bonds. The MDCAT Chemistry Unit 2 Live Test tests this heavily, so this guide covers every major topic.

The journey from Dalton's solid-sphere atom to our modern quantum mechanical model took over a century. Each model built on the last, each one explaining phenomena the previous model could not. For MDCAT, you need to know why each model was developed and which phenomena it explains.

Atomic Models: From Dalton to Schrödinger

Dalton's Model (1808)

Dalton proposed atoms were tiny, indivisible spheres. This explained law of constant composition but could not explain:

  • Electrical conductivity of matter
  • Radioactivity
  • Spectral lines of gases

Thomson's Model (1897)

After discovering the electron, Thomson proposed a "plum pudding" model: a sphere of positive charge with negative electrons embedded throughout. This accounted for electricity but failed to explain the massive concentration of positive charge in atoms.

Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911)

Rutherford's gold foil experiment showed most of an atom is empty space. Nearly all the mass is concentrated in a tiny, positively charged nucleus. Electrons orbit this nucleus in a cloud. However, Rutherford's model could not explain why orbiting electrons don't lose energy and spiral into the nucleus — a fatal flaw.

Bohr's Model (1913)

Bohr proposed electrons occupy fixed energy levels (shells) at specific distances. Electrons can jump between levels by absorbing or emitting energy. This explained the discrete spectral lines of hydrogen but failed for heavier atoms and could not explain chemical bonding.

Schrödinger's Quantum Mechanical Model (1926)

Schrödinger's wave equation describes the electron as a cloud of probability rather than a particle in a fixed orbit. An orbital is a region where the electron has a high probability of being found — not a path it follows. This model explains atomic structure, bonding, and chemical reactivity. This is the model MDCAT uses.

Key distinction for exams: A Bohr orbit is a fixed circular path (outdated). A quantum mechanical orbital is a probability distribution in 3D space (modern). Never confuse the two.

Quantum Numbers & Orbitals

Four quantum numbers fully describe the state of an electron in an atom. Each tells you something different:

Quantum Number Symbol Possible Values Tells You
Principal n 1, 2, 3, 4, ... Which shell; overall energy level
Azimuthal (angular momentum) l 0 to (n–1) Which subshell (s, p, d, f)
Magnetic ml –l to +l Orbital orientation in 3D space
Spin ms +½, –½ Electron spin direction

Orbital Shapes & Notation

  • s orbitals (l=0) — spherical; one orbital per shell (e.g., 1s, 2s)
  • p orbitals (l=1) — dumbbell-shaped; three per shell oriented on x, y, z axes (e.g., 2px, 2py, 2pz)
  • d orbitals (l=2) — cloverleaf or four-lobed shapes; five per shell (dxy, dxz, dyz, dx²–y², dz²)
  • f orbitals (l=3) — complex multi-lobed shapes; seven per shell
High-yield fact: The dx²–y² orbital has lobes on the x and y axes (not diagonal). The dz² orbital has a dumbbell on the z-axis plus a torus around the equator. These shapes are tested directly.

Electron Configuration

Writing electron configurations correctly requires following three principles:

1. Aufbau Principle

Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy. The approximate order is: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p, 7s, 5f, 6d, 7p.

Key exceptions: Chromium ([Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹) and copper ([Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹) violate the expected order because a half-filled or fully filled d subshell is more stable.

2. Pauli's Exclusion Principle

No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers. In practical terms: each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons, and they must have opposite spins.

3. Hund's Rule

When filling orbitals of equal energy (degenerate orbitals), electrons spread out to different orbitals with parallel spins before pairing. Example: nitrogen (N) has three p electrons (2p³) as ↑ ↑ ↑ in separate orbitals, not ↑↓ ↑ (which would give a lower stability).

Example Configurations

  • Sodium (Na, Z=11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹ or [Ne] 3s¹
  • Iron (Fe, Z=26): [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s² (note: 4s fills before 3d, but 4s loses electrons first in ions)
  • Gold (Au, Z=79): [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s¹ (a notable exception with filled f and d subshells)
Exam trap: When an atom ionizes, electrons are removed from the highest n value first, not the highest l value. Fe²⁺ is [Ar] 3d⁶, not [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s². The 4s electrons leave first even though they filled last.

Periodic Trends & Effective Nuclear Charge

Periodic trends arise from two competing forces: the attraction of electrons to the nucleus and the repulsion of electrons from each other. The key concept linking them all is effective nuclear charge (Zeff) — the net positive charge felt by an outer electron after inner electrons shield the full nuclear charge.

Atomic Radius

  • Across a period: radius decreases left to right. Nuclear charge increases, but shielding stays constant; outer electrons are pulled closer.
  • Down a group: radius increases. New electron shells are added, increasing shielding despite higher nuclear charge.

Ionization Energy

The energy required to remove the first electron from a neutral atom.

  • Across a period: increases. Higher Zeff holds electrons tighter.
  • Down a group: decreases. Outer electrons are farther from the nucleus and more shielded.
  • Exception: slight dips occur at Al (p¹ enters, easier to remove) and S (first d-orbital pairing, easier to remove).

Electronegativity

The tendency of an atom to attract electrons in a bond.

  • Across a period: increases (F is the highest at 4.0; H is 2.1).
  • Down a group: decreases (Li is 0.98; F is 4.0).

Electron Affinity

The energy released when an electron is added to a neutral atom.

  • Generally becomes more negative across a period (more attraction).
  • Becomes less negative down a group (electron is farther away).
  • Exception: Group 18 (noble gases) have very small/zero electron affinity because their outer shell is full.

Shielding & Penetration

Inner electrons reduce the effect of the nucleus on outer electrons (shielding). Penetration is how close an orbital can approach the nucleus. s and p orbitals penetrate better than d and f orbitals in the same shell. This is why, in the d-block, 4s electrons ionize before 3d even though 4s filled first.

Common MDCAT Mistakes in This Chapter

  1. Mixing up orbits and orbitals: Say "orbital" for the quantum model. "Orbit" is outdated Bohr language.
  2. Wrong electron order in ionization: Forgetting that 4s electrons leave before 3d. Fe²⁺ is 3d⁶, not 3d⁴ 4s².
  3. Confusing principal and azimuthal: n determines the shell; l determines the subshell type.
  4. Neglecting exceptions in electron configuration: Cr and Cu break the Aufbau order. Know them.
  5. Getting periodic trends backwards: Atomic radius and ionization energy trends are opposite as you go down a group.
  6. Misidentifying the d-orbital on axes: dx²–y² lies on the x and y axes; dz² has lobes on z and a torus.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the four quantum numbers and what do they represent?

The principal quantum number (n) determines the shell and overall energy; azimuthal (l) determines the subshell (s, p, d, f); magnetic (ml) determines orbital orientation in 3D space; spin (ms) indicates electron spin direction (±½). No two electrons can have identical sets of all four.

What is the difference between an orbital and an orbit?

An orbit (Bohr model) is a fixed circular path at a specific radius. An orbital (quantum model) is a probability distribution — a 3D region where an electron is likely to be found. The quantum model is more accurate and is what MDCAT expects you to use.

How do you write electron configurations correctly?

Follow three rules: (1) Aufbau principle — fill orbitals in order of increasing energy; (2) Pauli's exclusion principle — no two electrons with identical quantum numbers; (3) Hund's rule — electrons spread out to different orbitals with parallel spins before pairing. Watch for exceptions like Cr and Cu.

What are the periodic trends and why do they occur?

Ionization energy and electronegativity increase across a period and decrease down a group (higher nuclear charge pulls electrons tighter). Atomic radius and electron affinity show the opposite trend (distance and shielding increase down a group). All trends are driven by changes in effective nuclear charge.

What is the difference between shielding and penetration?

Shielding (or screening) is when inner electrons reduce the effective nuclear charge felt by outer electrons. Penetration is how close an orbital can approach the nucleus. s orbitals penetrate better than p, which penetrate better than d, in the same shell — which is why ionization removes 4s before 3d.

Why do Cr and Cu break the Aufbau principle?

Chromium ([Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹) and copper ([Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹) achieve extra stability with half-filled or fully filled d subshells. A single 4s electron is promoted to the 3d orbital to achieve d⁵ or d¹⁰. This is more stable than the "expected" d⁴ 4s² or d⁹ 4s² configuration.
