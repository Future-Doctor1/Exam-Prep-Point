1. Water: The Molecule of Life

Water forms almost three-quarters of the human body by weight and is the medium in which nearly every biochemical reaction takes place. Its behavior comes from one structural fact: the molecule is polar, with a partial negative charge on oxygen and partial positive charges on hydrogen, allowing water molecules to hydrogen-bond to one another.

  • Cohesion and adhesion — water molecules stick to each other and to other polar surfaces, allowing it to circulate through living bodies as a transport medium (e.g., in xylem).
  • High specific heat capacity — water resists rapid temperature change, which is why it moderates climate in aquatic environments and buffers body temperature.
  • High heat of vaporization — evaporative cooling (sweating) removes large amounts of heat with relatively little water loss.
  • Ice is less dense than liquid water — hydrogen bonds lock water molecules into an expanded lattice as they freeze, so ice floats.

For a deeper primer on water's chemistry before you move into organic molecules, the Khan Academy lesson on water, acids, and bases is a solid outside reference.

2. Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates follow the general formula Cx(H2O)y and are classified by size into three groups.

ClassUnitsExamples
Monosaccharides1 sugar unit (x = y in formula)Glucose, fructose, ribose
Disaccharides / Oligosaccharides2–10 unitsSucrose, lactose, maltose
Polysaccharides>10 unitsStarch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin

A frequently tested distinction is the type of glycosidic linkage: amylose (a component of starch) is joined by 1-4 glycosidic bonds, cellulose by 1-4 β-linkages that humans cannot digest, and glycogen by both 1-4 and 1-6 linkages that create its branched structure. Chitin, a nitrogen-containing polysaccharide, forms the exoskeleton of arthropods and the cell walls of fungi.

Sucrose is a common trap: it gives a negative Benedict's test because both its anomeric carbons are locked in the glycosidic bond, leaving no free reducing group.

3. Lipids

Lipids are a chemically diverse group united by one property: they are hydrophobic and largely insoluble in water.

  • Acylglycerols (fats/oils) — esters of glycerol and fatty acids, formed by condensation reactions. A triglyceride (three fatty acids + glycerol) is neutral and the body's most efficient energy store, since its carbons are more reduced than those in carbohydrates.
  • Phospholipids — the basic component of the plasma membrane's lipid bilayer; a hydrophilic phosphate head (its polarity comes from the phosphoric acid group) and hydrophobic fatty-acid tails. Lecithin (phosphatidylcholine) is the classic example, formed when phosphatidic acid combines with choline.
  • Steroids — built on a four-fused-ring backbone and contain no fatty acids; cholesterol and steroid hormones fall here.
  • Waxes — protective, water-repellent coatings on fruits, leaves, and exoskeletons.
  • Terpenoids — the only lipid group that occurs as true polymers (e.g., natural rubber, carotenoid pigments used as photosynthetic accessory pigments).

4. Proteins

Proteins are heteropolymers built from 20 standard amino acids, each differing only in its variable (R) group — the feature that gives each amino acid its individual chemical identity. Amino acids join through peptide bonds, formed by a condensation reaction between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next, releasing a water molecule.

Structure levelWhat it describes
PrimaryLinear sequence of amino acids
Secondaryα-helices and β-pleated sheets, stabilized by hydrogen bonds
TertiaryOverall 3D folding of a single polypeptide
QuaternaryAssembly of more than one polypeptide chain (e.g., hemoglobin); myoglobin, a single chain, has no quaternary structure

A classic applied question: sickle cell anaemia arises from a single amino acid substitution in the haemoglobin molecule — a reminder that primary structure alone can change protein function dramatically.

5. Nucleic Acids

DNA and RNA are polymers of nucleotides, each made of a pentose sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base. DNA uses deoxyribose and the bases A, T, G, C; RNA uses ribose and substitutes uracil for thymine. In DNA's double helix, purine always pairs with pyrimidine (A–T, G–C), which keeps the diameter of the helix constant along its length — a detail MDCAT likes to test directly.

6. Enzymes

Enzymes are protein catalysts that lower the activation energy of a reaction without being consumed by it. Substrates bind at the enzyme's active site, and two competing models describe how that binding happens:

  • Lock-and-key model — the active site's shape is rigid and pre-complementary to the substrate.
  • Induced-fit model — the active site is flexible and changes shape slightly as the substrate binds, improving catalytic fit. This is the model followed by regulatory enzymes.

Enzyme activity depends heavily on temperature and pH: activity rises to an optimum temperature and then falls sharply as heat denatures the protein, while pH affects the ionization of active-site residues. For a rigorous walkthrough of active-site chemistry and enzyme kinetics beyond MDCAT scope, see the NCBI Bookshelf chapter on enzyme biochemistry.

High-yield fact: A prosthetic group is a non-protein part of an enzyme that is permanently and covalently bound, while a coenzyme or cofactor binds loosely and reversibly — this distinction is tested almost every session.

Common MDCAT Mistakes in This Chapter

  1. Confusing hydrolysis (bond breaking, water added) with condensation (bond forming, water released) — always check which direction the reaction is going.
  2. Mixing up which polysaccharide belongs to plants (starch, cellulose) versus animals (glycogen) versus fungi/arthropods (chitin).
  3. Forgetting that triglycerides are not polymers, while proteins, carbohydrates, and nucleic acids are.
  4. Mislabeling insulin, hemoglobin, and myoglobin when asked which protein lacks quaternary structure.
  5. Overlooking that sucrose, unlike glucose or maltose, does not reduce Benedict's reagent.

Frequently Asked Questions

What topics are covered in MDCAT Biology Unit 2?

Unit 2, Biological Molecules, covers the properties of water, carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids, and enzymes — including their structure, classification, and biological roles.

Why is water considered a biological molecule in the MDCAT syllabus?

Water makes up most of living tissue by weight, and its polarity, hydrogen bonding, high specific heat, and cohesive-adhesive properties make it essential for temperature regulation, transport, and as the medium for cellular reactions.

What is the difference between the lock-and-key and induced-fit enzyme models?

The lock-and-key model treats the active site as a rigid shape that exactly matches the substrate, while the induced-fit model proposes the active site adjusts its shape as the substrate binds, improving the fit for catalysis.

How many amino acids are used to build human proteins?

Although far more amino acids exist in nature, only 20 standard amino acids serve as the building blocks for proteins in the human body.
