1. The Mole Concept

A mole is the SI unit for the amount of substance. One mole of any substance contains exactly 6.022 × 10²³ elementary particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) — this number is called Avogadro's number (NA).

Number of moles (n) = Given mass / Molar mass = Number of particles / NA
Example: How many moles are in 2.5 moles of NaCl expressed in grams? Since molar mass of NaCl = 58.5 g/mol, mass = 2.5 × 58.5 = 146.25 g.

2. Molar Mass & Molar Volume

Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance, expressed in g/mol, and is numerically equal to the atomic or molecular mass. Molar volume is the volume occupied by one mole of any gas at Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP) — this is 22.4 dm³ (22,400 cm³) for any ideal gas, regardless of its identity.

  • 1 mole of any gas at STP = 22.4 dm³
  • 1 amu = 1.661 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
  • Gram-atom refers to the molar mass of an element expressed in atoms rather than molecules

3. Empirical & Molecular Formula

The empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound, while the molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms in one molecule. The molecular formula is always a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

Molecular formula = (Empirical formula)ₙ, where n = Molecular mass / Empirical formula mass

Combustion analysis is the classic experimental technique used to determine the empirical formula of an organic compound by measuring the CO₂ and H₂O produced when a known mass of the sample is burned completely.

4. Stoichiometry & Balanced Equations

Stoichiometry is the quantitative study of reactants and products in a chemical reaction, based on the mole ratios given by a balanced chemical equation. It allows you to convert between mass, moles, and volume of any species in a reaction.

Example: For N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃, the mole ratio of N₂ : H₂ : NH₃ is 1 : 3 : 2 — this ratio is the backbone of every stoichiometry MCQ involving this reaction.

Remember: stoichiometric calculations always assume that reactants are converted completely into products according to the balanced equation — side reactions and reversibility are ignored for ideal calculations.

5. Limiting Reagent

The limiting reagent (or limiting reactant) is the reactant that gets completely consumed first, thereby stopping the reaction and determining the maximum amount of product formed. Any reactant left over is called the reactant in excess.

To identify the limiting reagent, divide the moles of each reactant by its coefficient in the balanced equation — the smallest value points to the limiting reagent.

6. Theoretical, Actual & Percentage Yield

  • Theoretical yield — the maximum amount of product calculated from the complete conversion of the limiting reagent.
  • Actual yield — the amount of product actually obtained in the lab (always ≤ theoretical yield due to side reactions, mechanical losses, or reversible reactions).
  • Percentage yield — how efficient the reaction was.
% Yield = (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) × 100

7. Combustion Analysis

Combustion analysis burns a hydrocarbon or organic sample completely in oxygen to produce CO₂ and H₂O only. The CO₂ produced is typically absorbed in 50% KOH, allowing chemists to back-calculate the moles of carbon and hydrogen in the original sample — and from there, the empirical formula. Note: metal oxides, alkyl halides, and amines cannot be reliably analyzed this way since they don't combust cleanly into just CO₂ and H₂O.

8. MDCAT Exam Tips for This Chapter

  1. Memorize NA = 6.022 × 10²³ and molar volume = 22.4 dm³ — they appear in nearly every question.
  2. Practice converting between mass ↔ moles ↔ number of particles ↔ volume (at STP) until it's automatic.
  3. Always write and balance the equation first before attempting limiting reagent or yield questions.
  4. Double-check units — MDCAT frequently uses unit-mismatch as a distractor (e.g., mg vs g, cm³ vs dm³).
  5. Practice past-paper style numericals repeatedly; this chapter rewards speed and accuracy more than memorization.
