The 6 Official Themes of MDCAT Logical Reasoning

According to the PMDC syllabus, Logical Reasoning is organized into six distinct themes, each with its own learning outcomes. Every one of the 9 MCQs is drawn from these six areas — nothing outside this list should appear on the actual paper.

THEME 5.1

Critical Thinking

This theme tests your ability to separate truth from falsehood and reasonable beliefs from unreasonable ones, using logic rather than assumption.

  • Build logical arguments to judge whether a statement is true or false
  • Support correct beliefs with sound reasoning
  • Spot and challenge false beliefs using logical evaluation
How to practice: Read short argumentative passages and ask "does the conclusion actually follow from the premise, or does it just sound convincing?" This is the single best habit for this theme.
THEME 5.2

Letter and Symbol Series

Sequences of letters, numbers, or symbols where each term follows a specific rule — arithmetic, geometric, or alphabetical.

  • Recognize arithmetic progressions within a number series
  • Recognize geometric progressions within a number series
  • Identify the rule behind a letter or symbol sequence
How to practice: Before jumping to the answer choices, write out the gaps between consecutive terms. Most series reveal their rule in the differences, not the numbers themselves.
THEME 5.3

Logical Deductions

Using given statements and the relationships between them to reach conclusions that are strictly implied — no outside assumptions allowed.

  • Predict new relationships from relationships already given
  • Build a new logical structure from information in an existing one
How to practice: Map relationships (family trees, rankings, seating orders) onto a quick diagram instead of holding them in your head — most errors here come from misremembering, not misreasoning.
THEME 5.4

Logical Problems

Puzzle-style questions where you combine several separate pieces of information to deductively arrive at one correct answer.

  • Use the outcome of one clue to unlock another
  • Apply structured elimination to solve multi-clue puzzles
How to practice: Eliminate options methodically rather than guessing — cross out what a clue rules out first, then work with what remains.
THEME 5.5

Course of Action

Given a stated problem or policy as fact, you decide which suggested administrative or corrective actions logically follow for resolving it.

  • Gather and organize the relevant parts of a scenario
  • Use that information to make a sound decision
  • Judge between competing proposed actions using logical argument
How to practice: A "course of action" must solve the stated problem directly and practically — reject options that are too extreme, address a different problem, or merely restate the problem.
THEME 5.6

Cause and Effect

Identifying genuine causal relationships between two statements — does one really explain the other, or is the link coincidental or reversed?

  • Explain events, incidents, and accidents with sound reasoning
  • Reject false beliefs by testing them against valid arguments
  • Distinguish a true cause from a shared unrelated factor
How to practice: Ask whether the "cause" statement could occur without the "effect" ever happening. If yes, it's probably not a true cause-and-effect pair.

A Simple 3-Week Study Plan for Logical Reasoning

Because this section rewards technique over content volume, a short, structured plan is more effective than open-ended practice. Here's a plan that fits around your Biology and Chemistry revision.

  1. Week 1 — Learn the rules: Go through all six themes once, understanding the type of question each one produces. Don't time yourself yet.
  2. Week 2 — Theme-wise drills: Practice 10–15 MCQs per theme per day, reviewing every mistake to identify which theme is weakest.
  3. Week 3 — Timed mixed sets: Attempt full 9-question mixed sets under exam timing (roughly one minute per question), simulating the real paper.
Daily minimum: Even 15–20 minutes a day of mixed Logical Reasoning and English practice is enough to keep both sections sharp without pulling time away from Biology and Chemistry.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many MCQs does Logical Reasoning carry in MDCAT 2026?

Logical Reasoning carries 9 MCQs out of the 180-MCQ MDCAT 2026 paper, making up 5% of the total score, per the PMDC Uniform Curriculum.

Is the MDCAT 2026 Logical Reasoning syllabus different from 2025?

No. PMDC has confirmed the MDCAT 2026 syllabus, including Logical Reasoning's six themes, is unchanged from 2025.

Which Logical Reasoning theme should I prepare first?

Letter and Symbol Series and Logical Deductions tend to have the clearest, most learnable rules, so most aspirants build confidence fastest by starting there before moving to the more judgment-based themes like Course of Action and Cause and Effect.

Do I need a separate book for Logical Reasoning?

Not necessarily a thick one — a focused MCQ bank aligned to the six PMDC themes, practiced daily in short sessions, is generally enough since the section tests reasoning skill rather than deep content knowledge.
