The 3 Skills Behind Every Reading Comprehension Question

The official PMDC syllabus defines "Reading and Thinking Skills" through three specific learning outcomes. Every Reading Comprehension MCQ on the paper is built around one of these.

SKILL 1

Scanning to Answer Short Questions

Scanning means searching the passage for a specific, locatable fact — a name, date, number, or short phrase — without reading every line.

  • Read the question first, then go hunting for the exact answer in the passage
  • Look for keywords from the question repeated in the text
  • Trust the passage over your memory — the answer is always stated or directly implied
How to practice: Time yourself finding one fact in a paragraph in under 15 seconds. Speed here comes from knowing what to ignore, not from reading faster.
SKILL 2

Deducing Meaning from Context

This tests whether you can work out what an unfamiliar word means using the sentence and paragraph around it, rather than a dictionary definition you may not know.

  • Look at the words immediately before and after the unfamiliar word
  • Check whether the sentence's tone is positive, negative, or neutral — the word usually matches it
  • Substitute your best guess back into the sentence and see if the meaning still holds
How to practice: When you hit an unknown word while reading anything in English this month, guess its meaning from context before checking a dictionary. This builds the exact instinct MDCAT tests.
SKILL 3

Analyzing Figurative Language

This covers how a writer or poet uses language that appeals to the senses — similes, metaphors, and personification — rather than literal statements.

  • Simile: compares two things directly using "like" or "as" (her voice was like a bell)
  • Metaphor: states one thing is another to transfer a quality (the classroom was a zoo)
  • Personification: gives human qualities to something non-human (the wind whispered through the trees)
How to practice: When a question asks "what effect does this phrase create," look for which sense it appeals to — sight, sound, touch — that's usually the intended effect.

Scanning vs. Skimming: Know the Difference

These two terms get confused constantly, and MDCAT passages reward using the right one at the right moment.

TechniqueWhat it's forWhen to use it
SkimmingGetting the general idea, tone, and structure of a passageFirst 20–30 seconds, before reading any question
ScanningFinding one specific fact to answer a direct questionAfter reading each question, hunting for its exact answer
The efficient order: Skim the whole passage once for its gist, then read each question and scan back into the passage for that specific answer. Reading the passage in full detail before seeing the questions wastes time you don't have.

Free Practice Test: 10 Reading Comprehension MCQs

Try this short passage-based set under timed conditions — aim for under 6 minutes total, which is roughly the pace MDCAT demands.

Passage: Long before hospitals had electric light, night-shift physicians relied on a single oil lamp to examine patients in the ward. The lamp's flame was a fickle companion — it guttered at the slightest draft, throwing shadows that danced across the walls like restless spirits. Yet doctors of that era developed a remarkable skill: they learned to read a patient's pallor and pulse by touch and dim light alone, a skill that modern diagnostic machines have made almost unnecessary. Dr. Zainab Rahim, who trained under one of the last physicians of that generation, still insists her students practice a full physical exam with the lights dimmed at least once during their training. "Machines can fail," she often tells them, "but a trained hand rarely does."
Q1 · Scanning

According to the passage, what did night-shift physicians once rely on for light?

  • A. A candle
  • B. A single oil lamp
  • C. An electric bulb
  • D. Moonlight through a window
Q2 · Scanning

Who insists her students practice exams with the lights dimmed?

  • A. A nurse supervisor
  • B. Dr. Zainab Rahim
  • C. A hospital administrator
  • D. The passage does not say
Q3 · Context clues

In this passage, "fickle" most nearly means:

  • A. Bright and steady
  • B. Unreliable and changeable
  • C. Expensive to maintain
  • D. Dangerous to handle
Q4 · Context clues

The word "guttered," as used here, most likely describes a flame that:

  • A. Burns unusually bright
  • B. Flickers weakly and nearly goes out
  • C. Produces thick smoke
  • D. Stays perfectly still
Q5 · Figurative language

"Shadows that danced across the walls like restless spirits" is an example of:

  • A. Metaphor
  • B. Simile
  • C. Personification
  • D. Alliteration
Q6 · Figurative language

Which sense does the phrase "shadows that danced" primarily appeal to?

  • A. Touch
  • B. Sound
  • C. Sight
  • D. Smell
Q7 · Inference

What can be inferred about Dr. Rahim's attitude toward diagnostic machines?

  • A. She believes they should replace physical exams entirely
  • B. She values them but doesn't want trained skill to disappear alongside them
  • C. She refuses to use them under any circumstance
  • D. She believes they are more accurate than any physician
Q8 · Main idea

Which sentence best captures the main idea of the passage?

  • A. Oil lamps were dangerous and should never have been used in hospitals
  • B. A hands-on diagnostic skill from the past is still worth preserving today
  • C. Modern medicine no longer needs physical examination skills
  • D. Dr. Rahim trained during the era before electric light existed
Q9 · Scanning

What does Dr. Rahim say can fail, unlike a trained hand?

  • A. Nurses
  • B. Oil lamps
  • C. Machines
  • D. Textbooks
Q10 · Context clues

The word "pallor," as used in the passage, most likely refers to:

  • A. A patient's heart rate
  • B. The paleness of a patient's skin
  • C. A patient's body temperature
  • D. A patient's breathing pattern

A Quick Study Plan for This Section

  1. Days 1–3 — Learn the three skills: Practice scanning, context-clue deduction, and figurative language identification separately, using short paragraphs.
  2. Days 4–7 — Mixed passages: Attempt full passage sets with all three question types mixed, timing yourself at roughly 40 seconds per question.
  3. Ongoing — Daily vocabulary in context: Read one short article a day and guess unfamiliar words from context before looking them up.

How many English MCQs are in MDCAT 2026?

What does the Reading and Thinking Skills competency cover?

It covers scanning a passage to answer short questions, deducing word meanings from context, and analyzing how a writer or poet uses figurative language to appeal to the senses.

What's the difference between scanning and skimming?

Scanning is searching for one specific fact to answer a direct question. Skimming is a quick first pass through a passage to grasp its general idea and structure before answering anything.
