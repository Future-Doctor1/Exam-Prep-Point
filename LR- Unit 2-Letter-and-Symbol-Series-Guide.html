1. Alphabet Position Values

Nearly every letter-series question relies on converting letters to numbers using their position in the alphabet — A=1 through Z=26. Memorizing this grid (or being able to count quickly from either end) is the single most useful skill in this chapter.

2. Letter Series Patterns

Letter series questions ask you to find the next term (or a missing term) in a sequence of letters or letter-groups. The three most common structures are:

Constant-step series

Each letter shifts by a fixed number of places. Two-letter or three-letter blocks often shift together.

Worked Example: ZX, WU, TR, QQ, ?
First letters step back by 3 each time: Z(26) → W(23) → T(20) → Q(17) → N(14).
Second letters also step back by 3: X(24) → U(21) → R(18) → Q(17) → L(12).
Next term = N + L = NL

Interleaved (two-track) series

What looks like one sequence is often two independent sequences woven together. Splitting the terms into odd and even positions is the fastest way to solve these.

Worked Example: JEQ, HEO, FEM, ?
First letters: J(10), H(8), F(6) — decreasing by 2 → next = D(4).
Middle letter: constant E.
Third letters: Q(17), O(15), M(13) — decreasing by 2 → next = K(11).
Answer = DEK

Conceptual / general-knowledge series

Some "letter" series aren't alphabet arithmetic at all — they're initials of a known list (planets, days, numbers). Always check for this before assuming a mathematical pattern.

Worked Example: M, V, E, M, ?
These are the first letters of the planets in order from the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter → answer = J

3. Number Series Patterns

Number series questions test whether you can identify arithmetic, geometric, or hybrid patterns quickly. The most frequently tested types on MDCAT past papers are Fibonacci-style addition series, alternating operation series, and "find the wrong term" series.

Pattern TypeRuleExample
FibonacciEach term = sum of previous two21, 34, 55, 89, 144, 233, 377
Alternating operations×2, then −8, repeating14, 28, 20, 40, 32, 64
Difference-of-differencesDifferences themselves follow a pattern (e.g. powers of 4)1, 2, 6, 22, 86 (diffs: 1, 4, 16, 64)

Find the Wrong Term

These questions hide a broken pattern inside a longer, correct one. The fastest approach is to split the series into two interleaved sub-series (odd-position and even-position terms) and check each independently.

Worked Example: 0, 2, 3, 5, 8, 10, 15, 18, 24, 26, 35
Odd positions: 0, 3, 8, 15, 24, 35 → these follow n²−1 (perfect pattern).
Even positions: 2, 5, 10, 18, 26 → should follow n²+1, giving 2, 5, 10, 17, 26 — but 18 appears instead of 17.
Wrong term = 18

4. Coding & Decoding

Coding-decoding questions apply a hidden rule (letter shift, digit shift, or reversal) to encode a word or number, then ask you to apply the same rule elsewhere. The key is to compare the original and coded versions position by position rather than trying to spot the "trick" all at once.

Worked Example: If 35796 is coded as 44887, decode 46823.
Compare digit-by-digit: 3→4 (+1), 5→4 (−1), 7→8 (+1), 9→8 (−1), 6→7 (+1). The rule alternates +1/−1 by position.
Apply to 46823: 4→5, 6→5, 8→9, 2→1, 3→4 → 55914

5. Symbol Substitution

These questions swap the meanings of mathematical operators (+, −, ×, ÷) and ask you to evaluate an expression using the swapped meanings. The safest method is to rewrite the entire expression with the real operations substituted in before calculating, respecting standard order of operations (BODMAS/PEMDAS) — see Math Is Fun's BODMAS guide for a refresher.

Worked Example: If '×' means addition, '÷' means subtraction, '+' means multiplication, and '−' means division, evaluate: 20 × 8 ÷ 8 − 4 ÷ 2
Rewrite: 20 + 8 − 8 ÷ 4 − 2 → divide first: 8÷4 = 2 → 20 + 8 − 2 − 2 = 24

6. Verbal Analogy & Classification

Analogy questions test whether you can identify the relationship between a pair of words and apply the same relationship elsewhere. Common relationship types include cause-and-effect, part-to-whole, and reward-for-action.

Worked Example: "Condolence" is related to "loss" in the same way "congratulation" is related to: Achievement
(Condolence is offered in response to a loss; congratulation is offered in response to an achievement.)

Classification (odd-one-out) questions work the opposite way: three or four items share a category, and one doesn't belong. Always identify the shared category explicitly before eliminating options — this avoids being misled by superficial similarities.

7. Common MDCAT Mistakes in This Chapter

  • Assuming every series is purely mathematical — always check for a conceptual pattern (planets, days of the week, numbers spelled out) before forcing an arithmetic rule.
  • Applying operator swaps inconsistently — rewrite the full expression with substituted symbols before calculating; don't swap operators "in your head" mid-calculation.
  • Ignoring order of operations after a symbol swap — BODMAS still applies to the new (swapped) operators.
  • Treating interleaved series as one sequence — when a pattern doesn't resolve cleanly, try splitting odd and even positions before giving up.
  • Rushing coding-decoding questions — compare digit-by-digit or letter-by-letter rather than guessing the rule from a glance.
