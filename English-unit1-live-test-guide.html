Why Subject-Verb Agreement Is High-Yield for MDCAT

Subject-verb agreement questions reward pattern recognition rather than deep reading comprehension, which makes them one of the more "learnable" parts of the English section relative to vocabulary or long-passage questions. Common trap patterns — collective nouns, "each/every," fractions, "neither...nor," inverted sentences after "never before" or "under no circumstances" — repeat across MDCAT past papers almost every year, so mastering these rules pays off directly on test day.

Example pattern tested: "Rarely ___ he make the same mistake twice" tests inversion after a negative adverb ("Rarely does he make..."), while "The Committee ___ appended a note to its report" tests whether a collective noun is treated as singular or plural — both classic MDCAT-style traps covered in this test.

Grammar Patterns Covered in This Test

  • Collective nouns & group subjects — Committee, Mayor and councillors, family, jury — singular vs. plural verb agreement.
  • Fractions & quantities — "Two-thirds of...," "Half of...," "Thirty pounds..." — agreement depends on the noun that follows.
  • "Each," "every," "many a," "one of" — near-universal singular-verb triggers that are easy to misjudge.
  • Correlative conjunctions — "not only...but also," "neither...nor," "as well as" — verb agrees with which subject?
  • Inversion after negative adverbials — "Rarely," "Never before," "Under no circumstances" — requiring auxiliary-first word order.
  • Spot-the-error format — identifying the specific underlined segment that breaks agreement in an otherwise correct sentence.
