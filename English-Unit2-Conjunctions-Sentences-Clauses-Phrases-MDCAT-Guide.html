1. Types of Sentences

MDCAT tests two overlapping sentence classifications: by purpose (declarative, imperative, interrogative, exclamatory) and by structure (simple, compound, complex, compound-complex). Structural classification is the one that trips up most students, so it deserves the closest attention.

TypeDefinitionExample
SimpleOne independent clause, no dependent clauseLife is what we make it. *(only if "what we make it" is treated as a complement, not a clause — see note below)*
CompoundTwo or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunctionOur hoard is little, but our hearts are great.
ComplexOne independent clause + at least one dependent clauseThe evil that men do lives after them.
Compound-ComplexTwo+ independent clauses + at least one dependent clauseWhen we won the championship, the captain jumped for joy, and the fans cheered.
Worked Example: "I cannot give you any money, for I have nothing."
"For" here functions as a coordinating conjunction (meaning "because"), joining two independent clauses — so this is a compound sentence, not complex. This is one of the most common MDCAT traps: "for" looks subordinating but behaves as coordinating (like FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So).

2. Clauses: Independent vs. Dependent

A clause has both a subject and a verb. The distinction that matters for MDCAT is whether it can stand alone:

  • Independent clause — expresses a complete thought and can stand as its own sentence.
  • Dependent (subordinate) clause — has a subject and verb but cannot stand alone; it depends on an independent clause for meaning.

3. Noun, Adjective & Adverb Clauses

Dependent clauses are classified by the grammatical role they play in the sentence — exactly the way a single word would function.

Noun Clause

Functions as a subject, object, or complement — anywhere a noun could go.

Example: I am happy that she is here.
"That she is here" is the complement of "happy" — a noun clause.

Adjective Clause

Modifies a noun or pronoun, usually introduced by who, whom, whose, which, or that.

Example: The teacher who teaches English is very kind.
"Who teaches English" modifies "teacher" — an adjective clause.

Adverb Clause

Modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb, and answers questions like when, why, how, or under what condition. MDCAT frequently asks you to identify the specific relationship the adverb clause expresses:

RelationshipCommon Signal WordsExample
Concessionalthough, though, even thoughAlthough he was tired, he kept working.
Reasonbecause, since, asI honour him because he is a brave man.
Conditionif, unless, provided thatUnless you tell me the truth, I shall punish you.
Manneras if, as thoughHe talks as if he knew everything.

4. Types of Phrases

A phrase, unlike a clause, has no subject-verb pair of its own — it's a group of words acting as a single unit within a sentence.

  • Noun Phrase — a noun plus its modifiers, functioning as subject or object.
  • Adjective Phrase — modifies a noun. Example: "The car with the broken window was parked outside" — "with the broken window" modifies "car."
  • Adverb Phrase — modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb. Example: "She moved with grace and dignity" modifies "moved."
  • Participle Phrase — begins with a present or past participle and functions as an adjective. Example: "Having finished his homework, he went out to play."
  • Absolute Phrase — modifies the entire sentence rather than a single word, often combining a noun and a participle.

For a deeper reference on distinguishing phrase types, see the Purdue OWL guide to phrases and clauses.

5. Conjunctions: Coordinating vs. Subordinating

Conjunction-fill questions are the most numerous question type in this chapter. The key is matching the logical relationship between clauses to the correct connector.

Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)

For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So — these join two independent clauses of equal grammatical weight.

Example: It is autumn, so the leaves are falling. (cause → effect, coordinating)

Subordinating Conjunctions

Because, although, since, unless, while, until, if, whether — these introduce a dependent clause and establish its relationship to the main clause.

Example: He not only lost his ticket, but also his luggage. (correlative conjunction pair: not only... but also)

Correlative pairs (not only...but also, either...or, neither...nor, both...and) are a favorite MDCAT trap because only one half of the pair is usually given, and you must supply the grammatically matching half.

6. Objects & Complements

These questions ask you to label the underlined part of a sentence — direct object, indirect object, or complement.

TermDefinitionExample
Direct ObjectReceives the action of the verb directlyMy father bought me a new phone.
Indirect ObjectThe recipient/beneficiary of the direct objectMy father bought me a new phone.
Object ComplementRenames or describes the direct objectThe committee made him captain.

7. Common MDCAT Mistakes in This Chapter

  • Misclassifying "for" and "so" as subordinating — both are coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS), so sentences using them are compound, not complex.
  • Confusing indirect object with object complement — an indirect object is a different entity receiving something; an object complement renames the direct object itself.
  • Treating every "that"-clause as an adjective clause — a "that" clause functioning as a subject or object is a noun clause, not an adjective clause.
  • Overlooking correlative conjunction pairs — "not only" almost always demands "but also" to complete the structure correctly.
  • Mixing up participle and gerund phrases — a participle phrase functions as an adjective; a gerund phrase functions as a noun.
