1. Newton's Laws of Motion

Sir Isaac Newton's three laws describe how forces affect the motion of objects, and they are the single most heavily tested topic across MDCAT physics past papers.

Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force. This is why a single unbalanced force cannot exist without producing a change in motion — a frequently tested concept.

Newton's Second Law

F = ma  →  a = F / m

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This single equation drives most numerical MDCAT questions on force and acceleration.

Worked Example: A force of 15 N acts on a 3 kg block on a frictionless surface. Find the acceleration.
a = F / m = 15 / 3 = 5 m/s²

Newton's Third Law

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Classic MDCAT examples include a swimmer pushing water backward to move forward, a person jumping off a boat, and recoil in firearms — all direct applications of this law.

2. Momentum and Impulse

Momentum (p) is the product of an object's mass and velocity, and it is a vector quantity — its direction always matches the direction of velocity.

p = mv  (unit: kg·m/s)

Impulse is the change in momentum produced by a force acting over a time interval:

Impulse = F × t = Δp = m(vf − vi)

Conservation of Momentum

In any closed system with no external force, total momentum before an event equals total momentum after it. This principle explains explosions, recoil, and every collision problem on the MDCAT syllabus — for a deeper mathematical treatment, see Khan Academy's momentum unit.

3. Collisions: Elastic, Inelastic & Perfectly Inelastic

Collision Type Momentum Kinetic Energy
ElasticConservedConserved
InelasticConservedNot conserved
Perfectly InelasticConservedLargest energy loss; bodies stick together
Worked Example: Object A (2 kg, 5 m/s) collides head-on and sticks to object B (3 kg, at rest).
Common velocity = (mAvA + mBvB) / (mA + mB) = (2×5 + 0) / 5 = 2 m/s

4. Coefficient of Restitution

The coefficient of restitution (e) measures how "bouncy" a collision is — the ratio of the relative velocity of separation to the relative velocity of approach.

e = (relative velocity after collision) / (relative velocity before collision)
  • e = 1 → perfectly elastic collision
  • 0 < e < 1 → real-world inelastic collisions
  • e = 0 → perfectly inelastic (bodies stick together)

5. Projectile Motion

Projectile motion combines constant horizontal velocity with uniformly accelerated vertical motion under gravity. It is one of the richest problem-solving areas on the MDCAT, closely tied to concepts in HyperPhysics's trajectory equations.

Time of flight: T = (2vi sinθ) / g
Maximum height: H = (vi² sin²θ) / 2g
Range: R = (vi² sin2θ) / g
Relation between T and H: T = √(8H / g)

Key Properties to Remember

  • Horizontal velocity (vicosθ) stays constant throughout the flight — there is no horizontal force acting (ignoring air resistance).
  • Vertical velocity is maximum at launch and zero at the peak of the trajectory.
  • Acceleration is always g (9.8 m/s²), directed downward, at every point of the flight — including at the highest point.
  • Complementary angles (e.g., 30° and 60°) launched at the same speed produce the same range but different times of flight and maximum heights.
  • The path traced by a projectile is a parabola.
Worked Example: A ball is thrown horizontally from a 45 m cliff at 10 m/s. Find its range.
Time to fall: t = √(2h/g) = √(2×45/10) = 3 s
Range = v × t = 10 × 3 = 30 m

6. Common MDCAT Mistakes in This Chapter

  • Confusing distance and displacement — a runner completing a full lap has covered distance but zero net displacement.
  • Assuming vertical velocity is zero at the peak means acceleration is also zero — acceleration due to gravity is never zero during flight.
  • Mixing up elastic vs. perfectly inelastic collisions — remember: momentum is always conserved, kinetic energy is not (except in elastic collisions).
  • Forgetting complementary angle range equality — 30°/60° and 20°/70° pairs always give equal range at the same launch speed.
